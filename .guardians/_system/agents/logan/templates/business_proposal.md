# Business Proposal Template (Sterling)

## Purpose
Create a professional, persuasive sales proposal for a target company that justifies pricing through clear ROI and value delivery.

**Input:** Project name + Target company information  
**Output:** `/.guardians/financial/proposals/<company>_proposal.md`

---

## Sterling's Proposal Writing Process

### Step 1: Gather Company Information
Before writing, Sterling asks clarifying questions:

**Required Information:**
- Company name
- Industry/sector
- Company size (employees, revenue if available)
- Primary pain points or challenges
- Current solution (if any)
- Decision maker(s) and their titles
- Budget considerations (if known)

**Questions Sterling Will Ask:**
```
To create a compelling proposal, I need to understand <Company Name> better:

1. What industry/sector are they in?
2. Approximate company size (employees and/or revenue)?
3. What's their primary pain point that <Project> solves?
4. What are they using currently? (competitor tool, manual process, nothing)
5. Who are the decision makers? (titles/roles)
6. Any known budget constraints or procurement process?
7. Timeline for decision/implementation?
8. Any unique requirements or concerns?
```

### Step 2: Research & Context
- Understand their industry norms
- Identify common pain points in their sector
- Research typical ROI expectations
- Consider their scale and use case

### Step 3: Build Value Narrative
- Map features to their specific pain points
- Quantify value in their context
- Frame price against their alternatives
- Make them the hero of the story

### Step 4: Write Proposal
- Executive-friendly language
- Lead with outcomes, not features
- Clear ROI calculation
- Professional but persuasive tone

## Sterling's Proposal Writing Guidelines

### Tone & Style

**Do:**
- Write for executives (clear, concise, business-focused)
- Lead with outcomes, not features
- Use specific numbers and calculations
- Tell a story (problem ‚Üí solution ‚Üí value)
- Make them the hero
- Be confident but not arrogant
- Show empathy for their challenges

**Don't:**
- Use technical jargon unnecessarily
- Lead with features ("Our tool has...")
- Make vague claims ("best in class," "world-class")
- Talk about yourself more than them
- Undersell the value
- Create FUD (fear, uncertainty, doubt) about competitors
- Be pushy or aggressive

---

### Language Patterns

**Instead of:** "Our software has automated reporting."  
**Say:** "Eliminate 10 hours of weekly manual reporting, freeing your team to focus on analysis and strategy."

**Instead of:** "We offer great customer support."  
**Say:** "When questions arise, our team responds within 4 hours, ensuring your operations never stall."

**Instead of:** "This will save you money."  
**Say:** "This delivers $400K in annual value for a one-time investment of $120K‚Äîa 3.3x return in year one alone."

**Instead of:** "Competitors can't do X."  
**Say:** "Unlike alternatives, <Project> enables you to <outcome they can't achieve elsewhere>."

---

### Framing the Price

**High-Value Frame:**
"For a one-time investment of $120K, <Company> gains a permanent asset that delivers $400K annually. That's like hiring three full-time employees at no ongoing cost."

**Comparison Frame:**
"Building this capability in-house would cost $350K and take 18 months. We deliver the same outcome today for $120K‚Äîsaving both money and time."

**Risk-Reduction Frame:**
"Every month without this solution costs <Company> $35K in continued inefficiency. The 4-month payback period means this investment pays for itself quickly."

**Opportunity Frame:**
"This unlocks a revenue stream currently inaccessible. If it generates even 20% of the projected $500K/year, it's already paid for itself."

---

### Handling Objections (Proactively)

**Objection: "That's expensive."**
**Preemptive response in proposal:**
"While $120K is a significant investment, consider the alternative: continuing to spend $400K annually on manual processes. The solution pays for itself in 4 months."

**Objection: "We could build this ourselves."**
**Preemptive response:**
"Internal development typically costs $350K+ and takes 12-18 months, with ongoing maintenance burden. <Project> delivers immediately and includes all updates."

**Objection: "We need to think about it."**
**Preemptive response (Why Act Now section):**
"Every month of delay costs $35K in continued inefficiency. Six months of 'thinking about it' equals $210K‚Äîmore than the entire investment."

**Objection: "What if it doesn't work for us?"**
**Preemptive response (Pilot Program):**
"Not ready to fully commit? Our pilot program lets you prove ROI on a limited scope before full investment."

## After Proposal Creation

### Sterling's Follow-Up Recommendations

**1. Review & Refine**
- Have manager review for accuracy
- Adjust tone for company culture if needed
- Verify all numbers and calculations
- Check for typos and formatting

**2. Delivery Strategy**
- Send as PDF (more professional than markdown)
- Include executive summary as email body
- Offer to present in person/video call
- Follow up within 3-5 business days

**3. Prepare for Questions**
- Anticipate objections
- Prep additional ROI scenarios
- Have case studies ready
- Be ready to adjust pricing/terms if needed

**4. Track & Learn**
- Note which proposals convert
- Identify common objections
- Refine value narratives based on feedback
- Improve future proposals
