How to Design Your Reward Economy
A mental framework for pricing chores, store items, and goals consistently.
Show guide
🏦 The two-currency system
🪙 10 coins = 💵 $1 — the recommended real-money exchange rate

Stewardwell uses two separate currencies on purpose:

🪙 Coins — personal rewards

Coins belong to the individual kid. They spend them in the Kid Store on personal rewards like screen time, a special snack, or choosing dinner. Coins foster individual responsibility.

⭐ Points — family teamwork

Points accumulate across the whole family toward Family Goals like a trip, a movie night out, or a new board game. Points foster collective effort — the whole family wins together.

🧹 Pricing chores

Base coin & point rewards on time and effort, not outcome. Consistency matters more than perfect numbers — adjust as you learn what motivates your kids.

Effort level Examples 🪙 Coins ⭐ Points
Quick (<5 min) Make bed, put dishes away, wipe counter 5 – 10 5 – 10
Medium (10–20 min) Vacuum, sweep + mop, clean bathroom sink 15 – 25 15 – 25
Hard (20–40 min) Clean whole bathroom, mow lawn, deep-clean kitchen 30 – 50 30 – 50
Big project (45+ min) Garage cleanout, yard work, wash the car 50 – 100 50 – 100

💡 Tip: Keep coins and points roughly equal on each chore. Diverge intentionally — e.g. give a solo-but-invisible chore more points to reward the family benefit.

🛍️ Pricing kid store rewards

Rewards should feel attainable but not trivial. A good rule of thumb: a kid doing 2–3 chores per day should be able to afford a small reward in 3–5 days, and a big reward in 2–3 weeks.

Small reward
50 – 150 coins

Choose tonight's dinner, special snack, stay up 30 min late

Medium reward
200 – 400 coins

Extra screen time day, rent a movie, small toy or craft supply

Big reward
500 – 1000 coins

Trip to the dollar store, new book, a special outing with a parent

💡 Tip: Don't set everything as a big reward. Having cheap wins keeps kids motivated early on.

🏆 Sizing family goals

Family goals use points, not coins. Set the point cost based on how long you want the goal to take the whole family to achieve — factoring in how many kids you have and how many chores they typically do.

Goal size Examples ⭐ Point cost Est. time (2 kids, avg effort)
Quick win Movie night, pizza night, game night 200 – 400 ~1 week
Medium goal New board game, drive-in movie, bowling trip 600 – 1000 2–3 weeks
Big goal Amusement park, weekend camping trip, new trampoline 1500 – 3000 1–2 months

💡 Tip: Always have at least one quick-win goal active. It keeps the momentum going while a bigger goal builds in the background.

⚡ Setting challenge difficulty

Challenges are one-time (or limited) special tasks that go beyond regular chores — reading goals, kindness streaks, earning a badge, etc. The difficulty tier sets expectations.

🥉 Bronze
25 – 50 coins · 25 – 50 pts

Single-day effort, simple behavior goals. Read for 20 min, help set the table all week.

🥈 Silver
75 – 150 coins · 75 – 150 pts

Multi-day or moderate difficulty. Practice an instrument 5 days in a row, read a full book.

🥇 Gold
200 – 400 coins · 200 – 400 pts

High effort or multi-week commitment. Learn to cook a meal, complete a project, a month-long streak.

💡 Tip: Use "requires proof" on gold and silver challenges so kids document their achievement — it's also a great keepsake.

✅ General tips
  • Start simple. Launch with 4–6 chores and 1–2 store items. Add more once your kids are engaged and you see what motivates them.
  • Calibrate after week one. If coins pile up with nothing to spend them on, add more store items. If kids never reach a reward, lower prices or add easier chores.
  • Never take coins away. Treat coins as earned wages. Consequences should live outside the coin system to keep it trustworthy.
  • Celebrate family goal completions. Make it a moment — announce it at dinner, let the kids pick the reward date. The anticipation is half the fun.
  • Pause, don't delete. When life gets busy, pause chores instead of deleting them. Your setup will be there when things settle down.
